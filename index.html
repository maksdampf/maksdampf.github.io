Maya Server

David Burkhardt Maya is a Server designed for Protonet GmbH by David Burkhardt. Protonet's approach to servers focuses on accessability. The goal is to enable anybody to run their server Infrastructure from within their office, without needing an IT-Operator to manage it. This led to a different design approach compared to traditional rack server chassis.

The device is cooled passively, without fans. Its interface consists only of one button that is surrounded by one status light that can express exceptions, such as updating, backup, rebooting, errors, via colored light animations.

As an all-metal design, small lots of a few hundred devices can be manufactured with craftsman level production methods such as cnc-lasercutting and hand-welding. More economical cnc-stamping and robot-welding are used for higher lot sizes of a few thousand.

This was not merely a design project, as Engineering and production ramp have also been implemented by David Burkhardt. This made it possible to introduce manufacturing tweaks and improve processes into the design. Development was done in an iterative fashion, without the necessity of consulting of other parties between the designer and contract manufacturers. Through these iterations it was possible to reduce manufacturing effort by over 50% during the design phase, while at the same time improving quality and lead time over the first generation design.

Patent DE 102013010867 B4

David Burkhardt A fanless cooling method was invented by David Burkhardt for a noiseless server design for Protonet GmbH. The method is based on the operating temperature range of each individual components and positioning these components according to it inside the chassis.

Heat from the processor, dissipates in a heatsink on top of the chassis. This generates an airflow by induced convection, that sucks in cold air from between the harddisks. These harddisks have the lowest temperature range and are thus positioned at the bottom of the chassis in the cooler intake airflow.

This working principle was benchtested against conventional cooling methods. The Patent was filed in 2013 and granted by DPMA in 08/2016.

Carla 2 Server

David Burkhardt & Tillmann Radmer This Server was designed for Protonet as a successor to their Carla Server of 2013.

It is not merely an update to the product line, but a complete technical redesign. All components are modular and can be slid out of the chassis individually. Reliability and independence in case of a power outage or network breakdown required additional plug-in modules.

Like the other products developed for Protonet, Carla 2 has been designed for accessability. It has only one button and unlike a rackmount Server it will quietly fit on an office desk. Its passive heatpipe cooling has been designed to fit newer, more powerful Hardware: 8 core, 16 thread Xeon processor, 128Gb of RAM and 50Terabyte of Harddisk-Storage. Modules for an integrated uninterruptable power supply and a fallback internet connection (LTE modem), can be integrated.

Process Engineering

David Burkhardt When designing for a startup company like Protonet, one challenge is to cope with a changing pace in development and manufacturing. What started as a test batch, had to be ramped up quickly to several thousand devices. To make this evolution possible, several new processes were developed by David Burkhardt for Protonet GmbH: Setting up ad-hoc or permanent production lines, designing additional tooling and software as well as creating instruction books and onboarding guides.

For example the design of rugged transport cases for parts supplied by subcontractors not only sped up the logistics process, but resulted in elimination of any damage caused by handling. It also led to an almost hundred percent decrease in packaging waste during manufacturing logistics.

Packaging Maya

David Burkhardt & Tobias Otto The packaging for the Maya Server of Protonet GmbH is a combined packaging for retail display as well as shipping.

The design inside is highlighting the product using slightly angled cardboard surfaces. This creates deeper shadows, more contrast and perceived three-dimensionality.

Prototype Jigs

David Burkhardt Design work for a startup company is about speed and iteration.

Using the concept of a minimum viable product (MVP), tooling for prototypes is quick, light and cheap. Constructed mostly from 3d printed parts, wood and little use of machined parts, these simple hand-operated jigs can be made in few hours or days of work. Compared to outsourced tool engineering, this saves weeks or months in lead time that are better spent on product design iterations.

Originally designed to only make up a single prototype, some of these tools made for Protonet served for much longer in volume production.

  • Protonet GmbH

Outdoor Furniture

David Burkhardt This set of outdoor furniture can be made from just two types of recycled wood. The design is inspired by Will Holman's Scrap Table (Guerilla furniture), but uses other proportions and simplified materials: generic 4x6 roof lath and 2x10 cm board.

The tapered legs are stressed to give necessary stiffness to the thin board. Contrary to Holmans design, the legs are not interconnected by threaded rods. Non-triangulated beams do not add much stability to the space frame, so they were left out. The design has been donated to public domain by David Burkhardt under the Free Art License.

Spring Scissors

David Burkhardt In 2007, a local scissors manufacturer wanted to launch a new retail brand for scissors. As an original manufacturer, the client already produced scissors for several well-known retail brands, but wanted to have a retail marque of their own. The client asked Prof. Glen Oliver Loew of the HfbK, to create a student team to come up with design ideas that would have recognition value.

Spring scissors are not new, in fact, they are some of the earliest examples of scissors in human history. But since then, the concept of a spring scissor, which stays open by itself and closes when pressed, has been reduced to a few niche applications like sheep-shearing.

Not rightly so: Scissor shears have unique strengths. It is easier to cut along curved shapes, since there is no link at the end of the blades.
