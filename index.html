Outdoor Furniture

David Burkhardt This set of outdoor furniture can be made from just two types of recycled wood. The design is inspired by Will Holman's Scrap Table (Guerilla furniture), but uses other proportions and simplified materials: generic 4x6 roof lath and 2x10 cm board.

The tapered legs are stressed to give necessary stiffness to the thin board. Contrary to Holmans design, the legs are not interconnected by threaded rods. Non-triangulated beams do not add much stability to the space frame, so they were left out.

Maya Server

David Burkhardt Maya is a Server designed and engineered by David Burkhardt for Protonet GmbH.

Protonet wanted a design that focuses on accessability. Their goal is to enable anybody to run server Infrastructure from within an office, without needing an IT-Operator to manage it. This led to a different design approach compared to traditional rack server chassis.

The device features passive, fanless cooling, combined with a one button physical interface. Status information is given by a luminous ring around the button that can display colored light animations for different system states.

From the beginning, David Burkhardt planned the design to be scalable in production from small lots of a few hundred to several thouthand devices. The all-metal design can be made using craftsman level production methods such as cnc-lasercutting and hand-welding. More economical cnc-stamping and robot-welding are used for higher lot sizes of a few thousand.

As David Burkhardt was also conducting engineering and production ramp, it was possible to introduce manufacturing tweaks and improve processes in the design.

Through design iterations and new volume oriented manufacturing processes, it was possible to reduce the cost of manufacturing by over 50% on later production models, while at the same time improving quality and lead time compared to earlier models.

Process Optimized Technical Drawings

David Burkhardt When working for Protonet GmbH, David Burkhardt designed several parts for volume manufacturing processes:

  • Aluminium extrusion of heatsink profiles
  • CNC-bending of heatpipe tubes
  • injection moulding of plastic parts
  • cold extrusion and die forging of custom bolts

Design for these processes differs from those for prototype production by accounting for special design rules, tolerances and custom technical drawing styles. For a seamless transition from prototype to volume manufacturing, these design rules were already applied in the design stage, sometimes with significant effect on the prototype designs.

Working for Protonet, this included choosing a manufacturer and working together with them to finalize the design, process schedule and production ramp.

Patent DE 102013010867 B4

David Burkhardt A fanless cooling method was invented by David Burkhardt for a noiseless server design for Protonet GmbH. The method is based on positioning indiviual components inside the chassis airflow according to their allowed operating temperature range.

Heat from the processor, dissipates via a heatpipe in a heatsink on top of the chassis. This generates an airflow by induced convection, that sucks in cold air from between the harddisks. These harddisks have the lowest temperature range and are thus positioned at the bottom of the chassis in the cooler intake airflow.

This working principle was bench-tested against conventional cooling methods. The Patent was filed in 2013 and granted by DPMA in 11/2015.

Carla 2 Server

David Burkhardt & Tillmann Radmer This Server was designed for Protonet as a successor to their Carla Server of 2013. Technical layout and thermal simulation were prepared by Tillman Radmer while David Burkhardt conducted concept, design, construction, production ramp and assembly instructions.

All components are modular and can be slid out of the chassis individually. The concept uses plug-in modules, like a battery backup unit and 4G-module, to increase reliability and independence in case of a power outage or cable network breakdown.

Several parts are shared with the other products developed for Protonet. Like those other products, Carla 2 features a passive cooling. In this Application the fanless cooling can handle up to 150Watts system power.

Process Engineering

David Burkhardt When designing for a startup company like Protonet, David Burkhardt was faced with ever changing pace in development and manufacturing. What started as a test batch, had to be ramped up quickly to several thousand devices. To make this evolution possible, several new processes were developed by David Burkhardt for Protonet:

  • Setting up ad-hoc or permanent production lines
  • designing additional tooling and software
  • creating assembly instruction documents and onboarding guides

For example David Burkhardt designed rugged transport cases with reusable foam inserts for parts and components. Protonet supplied subcontractor manufacturers with these cases to speed up logistics. Upon introduction of the cases, damage caused by handling, which was frequent before, did not occure anymore. It also led to a drastic decrease in packaging waste from manufacturing and logistics.

  • 2009-2016
  • Protonet GmbH

Packaging Maya

David Burkhardt & Tobias Otto The packaging for the Maya Server of Protonet GmbH is a combined packaging for retail display as well as shipping.

The design inside is highlighting the product using slightly angled cardboard surfaces. This creates deeper shadows, more contrast and perceived three-dimensionality.

Accessoires, like cables and the manual, are stored in the top and bottom half of the packaging. Even though very little PU-foam cushioning is used inside, the package passes drop tests at twice the height of DIN 22 248 standards.

Prototype Jigs

David Burkhardt When working for Protonet, David Burkhardt made several jigs for prototype tooling. They can be used to test assumptions, manufacturing processes or even for a pilot production run.

Prototype tooling was necessary because Protonet favoured a product development strategy of a 'minimum viable product'. This is a strategy is mostly used in modern Software Development, to gather insights with lower cost and risks, before developing all product features in detail.

Constructed mostly from 3d printed parts, wood and little use of machined parts, these simple hand-operated jigs can be made in few hours or days of work. Compared to outsourced tool engineering, this saves weeks or months in lead time that can be spent on product design iterations.

Originally designed to only make up a single prototype, some of these tools made for Protonet served for much longer in volume production.

  • 2012
  • Protonet GmbH

Spring Scissors

David Burkhardt In 2007, a local scissors manufacturer wanted to launch a new retail brand for scissors. As an original manufacturer, the client already produced scissors for several well-known retail brands, but wanted to have a retail marque of their own. The client asked Prof. Glen Oliver Loew of the HfbK, to create a student team to come up with design ideas that would have recognition value.

Spring scissors are not new, in fact, they are some of the earliest examples of scissors in human history. But since then, the concept of a spring scissor, which stays open by itself and closes when pressed, has been reduced to a few niche applications like sheep-shearing.

Not rightly so: Scissor shears have unique strengths. It is easier to cut along curved shapes, since there is no link at the end of the blades.

  • Scherenmanufaktur Paul GmbH

David Burkhardt is a product designer based in Hamburg, Germany. At the age of 17, he was co-founder of Netthelp, a student company offering IT-services for schools which is still operating today. Later, still in high school, he founded his own company for cnc-milled water cooling devices and small form factor computers that won several awards.

David then went on to study industrial design at the University of fine arts of Hamburg, in the class of Prof. Glen-Oliver Löw. During the time at HfbK he also worked as a tutor for the CAD-labs and Mixed Media Workshop. As of 2012 he joined Protonet GmbH, a Hamburg based startup that would set an european crowd funding record and get chosen by the Y-Combinator accelerator program. Until 2016 he was responsible for the design of every major Protonet product, its manufacturing and process development.
